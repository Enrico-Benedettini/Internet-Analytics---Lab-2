3. Power Law distribution¶

The distribution quite reminds us of what we have seen in class for the pareto law, thus we might check its CCDF (Complementary Cumulative Distribution Function) to verify if the type of the distribution matches our intuition. Indeed, it's also known that degree distributions often resemble power laws. To do this, we first compute the complementary data and then plot them

In [74]:
# First, compute the complementary data. To do this, we take a copy (to avoid modifications on the x values of degree_uniques)
# and we compute the cumulative values turning counts into a CDF.
counts = np.copy(degree_uniques)
for i in range(1, len(counts)):
    counts[i] += counts[i-1]
# Finally, we compute the max value and reverse the values of the CDF based on this ending up with our CCDF
counts_max = max(counts)
compl = [counts_max - n for n in counts]

# Plot the degree distributions but with y reversed according to our calculations
plt.loglog(degree_uniques, compl, color='red')
plt.title('CCDF of network1')
plt.xlabel('Degree d')
plt.ylabel('# of nodes with degree > d')
plt.show()

From this graph, we can easily see a very neat similarity with the CCDF of the power law distribution. We might then search for the $-\gamma$ value corresponding to the slope, but this already tells us the plot matches the power law distribution

def random_sample(G, sample_size=1000):
    # First, we take a sample of the graph
    nodes = list(G.nodes())
    sample_nodes = random.sample(nodes, sample_size)
    G_sample = G.subgraph(sample_nodes)
    path_lengths = [] 
    for a in G_sample.nodes: 
        shortest_paths = nx.shortest_path_length(G_sample,source=a) 
        path_lengths.extend([shortest_paths[b] for b in shortest_paths if b > a])
    return (path_lengths, G_sample)

# We pick the sample size to be 4000, a small but big enough value to actually have a reasonable sample of the graph

2. Comparison of the two networks¶

This two networks have a lot of differences. By simply looking at the data, we immediately see a difference in the values:

network 1 network 2
# of nodes 13033 26542
# of edges 18584 43516
min degree 1 1
max degree 1482 8
degree mean 2.8 3.2
degree median 1 3
degree sigma 20.8 0.9
degree variance 435 0.8
degree distribution type power law poisson
# of connected components 192 5
giant component # of nodes 12576 26481
giant component # of edges 18317 43437

By a fast look at the data, we see that first of all the second network is double in size, which might give more realistic data about it, since the more data we have, the better we can describe the network. The degrees of the first network have a very similar mean to the second one, as there's a difference of only 0.4 between 2.8 and 3.2. However, the variance in the first network is way bigger (435 to 0.9), which is again confirmed also by the max degree (1482 to 8).

Both networks have a giant component, but there's a huge difference in this as in terms of percentage over the total number of nodes, the giant component of network2 has $\frac{26481}{26542}*100 = 99.7701756\%$ of the total number of nodes.

Insted the giant component of network1 has $\frac{12576}{13033}*100 = 96.4935165\%$ of the total number of nodes. Both are quite high, but also considering that we have a fewer data for network1, this might change at the same dimension. Still, the giant component of network1 indeed includes less nodes than the giant component of network2. This is also shown by the fact that network1 has lots of connected components (192) compared to network2 (with only 5).

Finally, for the samples we took, which are supposed to be pretty well representative of the network, the average clustering coefficient C is quite similar, though higher for network1(0.018168888888888915 to 4.656660668824404), while the average shortest path length L of network2 is way bigger than the one of network1 (18.51965452357301 to 4.656660668824404). Indeed, as mentioned, we also know that network1 is a small-world network, while network2 isn't.

Exercise 2.6: Network identification¶

As we can understand easily thanks to the sampling and to the numbers of connected components, network1 represents a subgraph of routers comprising the Internet and network2 represents the network of roads around the city of New York. It's possible to understand it due to their composition and caracteristics:

  • network1: it's a small world network and routers networks are a typical example of small-world networks. Indeed, there we likely have some few routers that are connected to lots of points and most of them with very few connections (low degree) as also the degree distribution suggests.

  • network2: has very low degree, with a max degree of only 8. This comes from the fact that the mapping of roads can't produce any bigger node degree as a cross of more than 8 is physically impossible, so it would make a lot of sense. Also, the average is roughly 3.2, which makes even more sense for a cross to represent the node. Furthermore, pretty much evrything is connected, as we only have 5 connected components, which adds reason to our conclusion, as it's unlikely to have part of the city that are impossible to reach.
